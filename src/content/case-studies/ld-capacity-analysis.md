## Overview

An instructional designer was responsible for 13 curricula containing ~350 unique training assets. Maintenance requests arrived via Jira, but there was no data showing whether the workload was sustainable. Leadership assumed one designer could handle it. I built the quantitative case that proved otherwise — and gave the team a reusable model for making the same argument in the future.

---

## The Problem

Without a framework, capacity conversations were anecdotal: "I'm really busy" vs. leadership's implicit assumption that the queue was manageable. There was no shared language for discussing what it actually takes to maintain a training portfolio at scale — or what the business risk of deferring maintenance looks like in dollar terms.

"We'll get to it" becomes a lot harder to say when you can show exactly what "it" costs.

---

## My Approach

I built a **quantitative capacity model** from scratch across six phases:

**Phase 1: Portfolio Inventory** — Cataloged every training asset across 13 curricula — eLearning modules, ILT materials, facilitator guides, assessments, and job aids — by type and complexity.

**Phase 2: Maintenance Hour Estimation** — Applied industry-standard development ratios to estimate annual maintenance hours required per asset type.

**Phase 3: Capacity Calculation** — Calculated total portfolio maintenance need (~4,550 hours/year) against available designer hours (~1,100 hours/year after meetings, admin, and active project work).

**Phase 4: Business Language Translation** — Translated the gap into terms leadership cares about:
- Portfolio replacement value: $3.5M–$4.5M
- Hidden cost of deferred maintenance: $300K–$500K/year in content drift and rework
- Risk exposure by curriculum: which programs are most at-risk

**Phase 5: Per-Curriculum Breakdown** — Built a heat map showing maintenance gap by curriculum. One program needed ~920 hours/year of attention but was receiving ~48 hours — a **95% gap**.

**Phase 6: Repeatable Methodology** — Documented the full approach as a 6-phase process other designers on the team could apply to their own portfolios.

---

## Outcomes

- 📊 Quantified a **56% capacity gap** that had previously been invisible to leadership
- 🔄 Shifted the conversation from *"can you just update this?"* to *"here's what we're choosing not to maintain"*
- 📐 The analysis framework was documented as a **repeatable 6-phase methodology** for the team
- 🏢 Informed headcount and prioritization discussions at the leadership level
