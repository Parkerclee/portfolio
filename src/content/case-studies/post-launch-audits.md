## Overview

An L&D team consistently produced accurate, high-quality eLearning — but the system around it kept breaking. Training would go live without a single source of truth. Edge cases discovered post-launch lived only in Slack. Nobody was measuring whether the *delivery model* worked, only whether the content was correct. I built a rubric to evaluate both, then applied it retroactively to two high-profile launches.

---

## The Problem

Content quality alone wasn't the issue. The failures were systemic: no consolidated reference page for agents, business-unit-created materials (huddle guides, tip sheets) existing in parallel but disconnected from formal training, and no feedback loop to capture what agents actually needed in the first 30 days post-launch.

Good content was going out into a broken delivery infrastructure — and since the content scored fine, nobody was looking at the infrastructure.

---

## My Approach

I developed a **100-point training effectiveness rubric** and applied it retroactively to two high-profile product launches.

### The Rubric — 5 Dimensions

| Dimension | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **Content quality** | Accuracy, learning objectives, scenario-based practice, knowledge checks |
| **Delivery timing** | Training-to-launch gap, phased rollout alignment, assignment sequencing |
| **Source of truth** | Single consolidated reference page vs. scattered materials |
| **Feedback loops** | Post-launch monitoring, Slack signal capture, content update cadence |
| **Cross-BU coordination** | Were all affected teams trained, or just the primary audience? |

### For Each Launch, I:

- Scored every training asset against the rubric
- Analyzed **200+ agent communication messages** for post-launch confusion signals
- Mapped the gap between what was trained and what agents actually needed in the first 30 days
- Produced a retrospective **Readiness Guide** — the page that should have existed at launch — as both a reference artifact and a template for future use

---

## Outcomes

- 🎯 **Launch A scored 79/100 on content** — but had zero source-of-truth pages and 3+ workarounds living only in chat channels, proving the delivery model was the failure point, not the content
- ⚠️ **Launch B scored 55/100 overall**, revealing 5 concurrent changes bundled without coordinated training and a display bug affecting ~2M users that was never addressed in training materials
- 🔁 The rubric became a **reusable evaluation tool** for future launches
- 📋 The retrospective readiness pages became the template for a new standard: **every launch above Tier 4 now requires a readiness page before go-live**

Evaluation design beyond Level 1 happy sheets — Kirkpatrick applied practically. The ability to prove a systemic problem with data, not intuition. Strategic influence: turning a retrospective into a standing organizational process. Comfort operating in ambiguity and building structure where none exists.
